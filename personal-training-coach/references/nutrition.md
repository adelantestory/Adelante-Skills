# Nutrition Reference

## Protein Targets By Goal

| Goal | Protein (g per lb bodyweight) |
|---|---|
| Muscle building | 1.0–1.2g |
| Body recomposition | 0.9–1.1g |
| Weight loss | 1.0–1.2g (higher preserves muscle in deficit) |
| Strength building | 0.8–1.0g |
| Endurance | 0.7–0.9g |
| General health | 0.7–0.9g |

Older adults (55+): Add 0.1–0.2g per lb above the above targets. Muscle protein synthesis is less efficient with age.

## Calorie Targets By Goal

### Step 1 — Estimate Maintenance (TDEE)
- Sedentary (desk job, light activity): bodyweight × 14–15
- Moderately active (3–4 training days): bodyweight × 15–16
- Active (5–6 training days): bodyweight × 16–17

### Step 2 — Apply Goal Adjustment
- Muscle building: TDEE + 200–300 cal (lean bulk)
- Body recomposition: TDEE (maintenance) ±100 cal
- Weight loss: TDEE − 300–500 cal (target 0.5–1 lb/week loss)
- Strength building: TDEE + 100–200 cal
- Endurance: TDEE + fuel for training volume (variable)
- General health: TDEE to slight deficit depending on body composition

### Step 3 — Calculate Carbs and Fat
1. Protein calories = protein grams × 4
2. Fat = 25–35% of total calories ÷ 9 = fat grams
3. Carbohydrate calories = total calories − protein calories − fat calories
4. Carbohydrate grams = carb calories ÷ 4

## Meal Structure

### How Many Meals
Adapt to the person's lifestyle and preference — there is no universally correct number. Common structures:
- **3 meals** — suits people who prefer simplicity or have limited eating windows
- **4 meals** — good balance for most training schedules
- **5 meals** — useful when protein target is hard to hit in fewer sittings, or when pre-workout timing matters

The priority is hitting daily protein and calorie targets consistently. Meal frequency is secondary to that.

### Template Structure (adapt as needed)
1. Breakfast — protein + carbs + fat
2. Lunch — main protein meal + complex carbs + vegetables
3. Pre-workout (training days, 60–90 min before) — light protein + fast carbs
4. Dinner — protein + carbs + vegetables
5. Before bed — casein protein (cottage cheese, Greek yogurt) — especially valuable for muscle building and recomposition goals

### Rest Day Adjustment
On rest days remove the pre-workout meal and reduce carbohydrates slightly — typically 150–250 calories of carbohydrates. Protein stays constant every day without exception.

### Never On Any Day
- Reduce protein below target to save calories
- Skip the pre-workout meal before a strength session — strength training fasted impairs performance and recovery

## Food Sources

**Protein (whole food priority):**
Chicken breast, turkey, eggs, cottage cheese, Greek yogurt, fish, lean beef, beans + grains (complete protein), whey protein as supplement

**Carbohydrates:**
Brown rice, oats, sweet potato, whole grain bread, fruit, beans, vegetables

**Fats (anti-inflammatory priority):**
Olive oil, avocado, nuts, eggs, fatty fish

---

## Timing Principles

- **Pre-workout:** For strength and resistance training, eat protein + carbs 60–90 min before the session — fasted strength training impairs performance and recovery. For light cardio or endurance work, fasted training is acceptable for some people and goals — use personal preference and energy levels as the guide.
- **Post-workout:** Protein + carbs within 30–60 min of finishing a strength session. Pre-workout protein counts toward this window if timing overlaps.
- **Before bed:** Casein protein for overnight muscle synthesis. Cottage cheese or Greek yogurt.
- **Creatine:** 5g daily. Timing flexible — consistency matters more than timing. Taking with a meal improves absorption.

## Calorie Adjustment Rules

Monitor weekly weight averages. Apply adjustments based on goal:

**Muscle building:**
- Flat 2+ weeks → +100–150 cal (add carbs)
- Gaining faster than 1 lb/week → −150 cal carbs
- Waist increasing without weight gain → −150 cal carbs

**Weight loss:**
- Stall 2+ weeks → −150–200 cal (reduce carbs first)
- Losing faster than 1 lb/week → +100 cal (protect muscle)
- Energy very low → Increase protein, check meal timing

**Recomposition:**
- Rapid weight gain → −100 cal carbs
- Rapid weight loss → +100 cal carbs
- Target is stable weight with slowly improving measurements

## Travel Nutrition Strategy

**General principles:**
- Pack portable protein sources that don't require refrigeration — protein bars, protein powder packets, jerky, or shelf-stable options available in your region
- On arrival, find a local grocery store and buy easy high-protein foods for your room (yogurt, eggs, cheese, or local equivalents)
- At restaurants, always anchor the meal around a quality protein source — grilled, baked, or steamed — with a starch and vegetables
- Ask for sauces and dressings on the side to control hidden calories
- Avoid meals dominated by refined carbs with no protein anchor

**What to pack (adapt to your region and dietary preferences):**
- Portable protein bars or equivalent — enough for one per day of travel
- Protein powder in single-serve packets or a small container
- Mixed nuts — pre-portioned, useful for filling protein and fat gaps
- Any daily supplements — creatine, vitamins

**At restaurants (universal strategy):**
- Order grilled, baked, or steamed protein as the centerpiece
- Request extra protein if portion sizes are small
- Substitute fried sides for salad, vegetables, or a starch that fits your targets
- Avoid cream sauces, heavy dressings, and deep-fried proteins

**Travel day minimum targets:** 75% of normal protein · Daily creatine · Casein-equivalent protein before bed

## Supplement Recommendations

**Essential:**
- Creatine monohydrate 5g daily — most research-backed supplement. Supports muscle, strength, cognition, and bone density.

**Useful:**
- Whey protein — convenient protein source when whole food is impractical
- Vitamin D3 — especially for low-sun environments

**Not necessary:**
- Pre-workout stimulants — unnecessary if meal timing is correct
- BCAAs — redundant with adequate total protein intake
- Most other supplements — not supported by strong evidence
